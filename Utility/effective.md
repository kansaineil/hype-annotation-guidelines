## **Effective — annotation guidance**

Adjective: **effective**  
Category: **UTILITY**

---

### **1. Value-judgement**

Usually YES — *effective* positively evaluates something as working.

NO when used in technical or domain-specific senses where *effective* means “actual” or “operational” rather than evaluative.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

**YES**

- *Such knowledge will contribute to development of **effective** prevention and treatment approaches.* (YES)

**NO**

- *Tests will be made of changes in the size of the **effective** visual field.* (NO)

</details>

---

### **2. Hyperbolic**

No.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

NA

</details>

---

### **3a. Gratuitous**

Mixed.

Mark YES when *effective* can be deleted without changing the core claim, especially in broad goal phrases such as *develop effective treatments/strategies/approaches*.

Mark NO when *effective* is the main result, comparison, condition, or technical descriptor, especially in predicate position (*X is effective*), comparative/superlative forms (*more/most effective*), or negated need/gap statements (*no effective treatment*).

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

**YES**

- *Such knowledge will contribute to development of **effective** prevention and treatment approaches.* → proposition remains: development of prevention and treatment approaches.

- *This project has three specific aims … aid in the design of **effective** interventions to prevent or alleviate impairment.*

- *The eventual purpose of this study will be to develop **effective** parent training procedures.*

**NO**

- *Functional neuromuscular stimulation has previously been demonstrated to be **effective**.*

- *Though **effective** in most cases, this treatment has significant drawbacks.*

- *No **effective** treatment has been established.*

- *The treatment program will be compared as to which training model is most **effective**.*

- *Pilot studies at other institutions suggest that the drug may be very **effective**.*

</details>

---

### **3b. Amplified**

Often amplified by comparative/superlative and degree markers: *more effective, most effective, very effective, completely effective, equally effective, markedly more effective.*

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *Pilot studies at other institutions suggest that the drug may be very **effective**.*

</details>

---

### **3c. Broader context**

Often YES — frequently appears in promotional utility stacks with *safe, efficient, cost-effective, long-lasting, rapid, useful, specific, selective,* or *safer.*

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *We will build upon our extensive prior experience with our highly **effective**, scalable Hub and Spoke system based out of the University of Cincinnati, College of Medicine.*

</details>

---

### **3d. Grounds**

Often YES. Often justified through evidence, prior demonstrations, comparisons, experimental findings, or stated conditions of use.

Mark YES when reasons are given for why something is effective or under what circumstances it is effective.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *Functional neuromuscular stimulation has previously been demonstrated to be **effective** by employing chronically indwelling percutaneous electrodes.*

- *HPD’s can indeed be **effective** in curbing audition problems if selected and used properly.*

- *Pilot studies at other institutions suggest that the drug may be very **effective**.*

</details>

---

### **3e. Relative / Hedged**

Common. Frequent in comparative, conditional, negated, modal, or limited claims.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *If found **effective**, we anticipate that the intervention techniques could be readily adapted.*

- *Though **effective** in most cases, this treatment has significant drawbacks.*

</details>
