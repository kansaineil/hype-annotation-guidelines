## **Generalizable — annotation guidance**

Adjective: **generalizable**  
Category: **UTILITY**

---

### **1. Value-judgement**

Usually YES — positively evaluates transferability beyond the immediate study.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *The facilitated communication intervention is designed to be easily **generalizable** to other hospitals and ICUs and, if successful, would improve the quality of care.*

</details>

---

### **2. Hyperbolic**

No.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

NA

</details>

---

### **3a. Gratuitous**

Mixed. Use the deletion test strictly.

Mark YES when *generalizable* can be removed without changing the core claim.

Mark NO when generalizability is the central claim, especially in predicate uses, negation, questions, or when the scope of transfer is specified.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

**YES**

- *The goal of the project is to provide **generalizable** information about the implementation of practice redesign strategies.*

**NO**

- *Work on HIV-1 Clade B infection and Caucasian populations therefore may not be **generalizable** to HIV clades and human populations internationally.*

</details>

---

### **3b. Amplified**

Sometimes YES — clear boosters include *highly, widely, broadly, readily, easily,* and *most widely.*

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *The lessons learned in applying siRNA technology for treatment of PC should be readily **generalizable** to a host of other dominant skin disorders and likely to other disorders resulting from mutated or overexpressed disease-causing genes.*

</details>

---

### **3c. Broader context**

Often YES — frequently stacked with utility/promotional terms such as *effective, sustainable, scalable, applicable, portable, robust, flexible, valid, simple.*

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *The decision support tool will be utilized in order to ensure that the proposed approach is **generalizable**, portable, and scalable.*

</details>

---

### **3d. Grounds**

Often YES — when transferability is justified through study design, validation, diverse samples, or supporting evidence.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *The results will be **generalizable** to the US adult population due to the study cohort sampling.*

- *We will validate the gene expression… after noting that single-center data may not be **generalizable** to other centers.*

- *Combining data from the three BCERC centers will improve external validity, making the findings more **generalizable** to all girls in the United States.*

</details>

---

### **3e. Relative / Hedged**

Common — often appears with *may, might, should, potentially, more, not*, questions, or specified limits.

<details markdown="1">
<summary><em>Examples</em></summary>

- *These findings may be **generalizable** to other rural and Appalachian communities in the United States.*

- *How **generalizable** are the program effects across participants, intervention staff, settings and time?*

- *Data from our single-center may not be **generalizable** to other centers.*

</details>
